Research Basics:
Searches & Sources

Two skills carry an inquiry: building sophisticated search strings, and appraising a medley of potential sources. This guide walks you through both, then asks you to practice.

Our Goals

Two skills carry an inquiry: building sophisticated search strings and appraising a medley of potential sources.

Application

I can construct sophisticated search strings.

Combine keywords, Boolean operators, and modifiers to ask a search tool for exactly what you need.

Evaluation

I can appraise a medley of potential sources.

Identify source types and interrogate them for accuracy, credibility, relevance, and intent.

Building Effective Searches

Your search skills determine how many high-quality, relevant sources you'll encounter while conducting research. A sophisticated search boasts three components.

Quality Keywords

Your keyword choices form the foundation of a search. The stronger your foundation, the better your results!

Boolean Logic

Boolean operators are a form of algebraic logic that empower you to expand or narrow a search.

Modifiers

These symbols and characters perform special functions, allowing you to execute more efficient searches.

Determining Keywords

Keywords are the words and phrases we use to search for information about a topic. When you type a series of words or phrases into a search box, we call it a search string.

Most people conduct conversations using natural language, a system of communication that develops without premeditation and forms rules through regular use.

Most computational systems use artificial or constructed languages. These systems are devised or premeditated. They follow set rules and norms that differ from the grammatical and syntactical tendencies of natural language in significant ways. LLMs aren’t trained on these principles, but learn them through inference based on statistical correlations.

Search functions are not chatbots! You'll retrieve better results if you speak to them in their native language!

Your first swing at a search is unlikely to yield the sort of results that can sustain a thoughtful inquiry. Artisanal searches take time to develop!

  • Initial Terms: What words come to mind when you think about your topic? How would ordinary people identify it in an everyday conversation?
  • Synonyms and Alternative Terms: Grab a thesaurus! What other words or phrases might you use to refer to this topic?
  • Formal Language: Experts tend to use specialized language. What terms or phrases come up in academic writing on the topic?
  • Top Level Guides and Subject Headings: Databases often feature clickable guides that introduce available content. They also rely on metadata to point users to related materials within articles.

Boolean Operators

    
Use Boolean operators judiciously to spice up a simple search! Be sure to enter these terms in ALL CAPS.

    

      

        
AND

        
Narrows results by returning BOTH keywords.

        
chicken AND salad
tuna AND salad

      

      

        
OR

        
Broadens results by returning EITHER keyword.

        
chicken OR tuna
salad OR melt

      

      

        
NOT

        
Narrows results by EXCLUDING a keyword.

        
chicken NOT tuna
salad NOT tuna

      

    


    
Special Searches and Modifiers

    
Combine keywords and modifiers to expand or narrow your search!

    

      

        
Quotation Marks " "

        
Returns exact phrases.

        
"chicken salad"
"tuna salad"
"tuna melt"

      

      

        
Parentheses ( )

        
Prioritizes enclosed terms (just like any equation!).

        
(chicken OR tuna) salad
(chicken OR tuna) melt

      

      

        
Wildcard *

        
Truncates, or shortens, a term, including all possible tenses, compounds, and variations.

        
cook*

      

    


    
Savvy Searching

    
How do we combine keywords and modifiers to create truly sophisticated search strings?

    

      

        
Combining Terms

        
"(chicken OR tuna) salad" AND recipe

        
    
          
  • It's the Distributive Property, but for search!
    • 
          
  • Prioritizes parentheses, searching for the respective keywords "chicken" and "tuna" exclusively.
    • 
          
  • Only pulls results that contain the exact phrases "chicken salad" or "tuna salad" AND the word "recipe".
    • 
        

      

      

        
Wildcards and Grouped Synonyms

        
child* AND (psycho* OR psychia*)

        
    
          
  • This is a large but manageable search!
    • 
          
  • Locates all variations on the words child- (e.g. child, children, childhood), psycho- (e.g. psychology, psychotherapy, psychopath), and psychia- (e.g. psychiatry, psychiatric, etc.).
    • 
          
  • Restricts results to content containing both a child- variant AND either a psycho- OR psychia- variant.
    •

Evaluating Results

    
How do you evaluate an informational resource for accuracy, objectivity, credibility, relevance, and all those other essential criteria? It begins with knowing what you're looking at.


    
Media literacy is the ability to access, analyze, evaluate, create, and act using all forms of communication.

    

      
It is an essential skill in our era, when information is abundant and comes from a multitude of sources. Critical information consumers both recognize common media formats and understand the differences between source types. They can capably identify and critically assess the information they encounter.

      
Definition adapted from the National Association for Media Literacy Education (NAMLE), "Media Literacy Defined."

    


    
Three Source Types

    

      

        Regular / Traditional
        
Regular media intend to inform, entertain, or persuade a broad audience. They tend to prioritize timeliness and accessibility, but sometimes lack depth and rigorous fact-checking. Regular media are typically produced by journalists or content creators who may lack subject matter expertise. Formats include newspapers, magazines, television, radio, and digital platforms like blogs and social media.

      

      

        Academic / Scholarly
        
Academic or scholarly media are created by experts in a specific field and are intended for an audience of other experts, researchers, or students. These resources include peer-reviewed journal articles, academic books, and conference papers. They undergo a rigorous review process to ensure accuracy and credibility. Scholarly media are characterized by detailed analysis, extensive references, and use of technical language.

      

      

        Grey Literature
        
Grey literature falls somewhere between regular media and scholarly resources. It includes materials such as government reports, white papers, policy briefs, and technical documents that are not published through traditional commercial or academic channels. Grey literature is often produced by organizations, think tanks, or government agencies and can provide valuable insights and data. However, it may not always undergo the same level of peer review as scholarly resources, so it's important to critically evaluate its credibility and relevance.

      

    


    
Primary or Secondary?

    
Whatever the subject, labeling a source primary or secondary will allow you to engage with it more skillfully.

    

      

        
Primary

        
Primary sources provide firsthand information created during the era under consideration. Primary sources exist in the context of their time. By interpreting them, we aspire to understand directly.

      

      

        
Secondary

        
Secondary sources attempt to retell, interpret, or analyze a topic or subject. Secondary sources help us make broader sense of a topic by creating a larger narrative or story about it.

      

    


    

      
A Classification Caveat

      
The same source can be primary in one context and secondary in another. A 1960 textbook chapter on World War II is secondary for a student researching the war itself. For a student researching how postwar educators understood and taught recent catastrophe to mid-century children, that same chapter becomes primary evidence of period thinking. Before you assign a label, ask what you are actually investigating.

Skim or Scan?

    
Informational abundance can feel overwhelming! When you encounter a new source, elect to skim or scan to get started.

    

      

        
Skimming

        
Skimming allows you to process a large amount of information quickly. To skim, read the first and last sentence in a paragraph. If you don't understand how the author moved between the two ideas, go back and read until it makes sense.

      

      

        
Scanning

        
Scanning targets specific bits of information. To scan, search your article for specific words and phrases. (CTRL-F is a timesaver, but you might miss slight variations or suffer OCR issues, especially with a large document or a PDF.)

      

    

  







  

    
The Three Cs

    
Evaluating information is all about due diligence. If you ask questions related to these three broad categories, you're bound to address important considerations like accuracy, provenance, relevance, objectivity, agenda, and intended purpose.


    

      

        
Creator

        
Who made it? When? Why?

        
    
          
  • Who created this source? Is an author or authorial group identified?
    • 
          
  • What are the creator's academic and/or professional credentials? Are they a recognized expert in their field? Who or what makes them an authority?
    • 
          
  • Is the creator affiliated with institutions or organizations? What other connections are revealed by a cursory search?
    • 
        

      

      

        
Content

        
What does it say? How does it say it?

        
    
          
  • Does the content provide ample attributions and citations? Are its sources easy to locate and verify?
    • 
          
  • How does this information square with your prior knowledge about and other sources on the subject? Is it reasonable?
    • 
          
  • How is the material written and organized? Whom is it addressing? What questions does it strive to answer? What positions does it take?
    • 
          
  • When was the content last updated?
    • 
          
  • When was it first created? If it's digital, do links and multimedia function properly?
    • 
        

      

      

        
Context

        
Where did you find it? How is it situated?

        
    
          
  • Where, why, how, and by whom was this content disseminated?
    • 
          
  • Who funded its creation?
    • 
          
  • Where and how did you find it?
    • 
          
  • What other materials exist alongside it?
    • 
          
  • Can you locate reviews of the content or its creator?
    • 
          
  • Why does it exist? Whose agenda(s) does it serve?

Reading Academic Articles


    
Reading is not a passive activity. It is a skill. It can be learned.

    
Notetaking is not intuitive. It is a skill. It can be learned.


    
Before You Begin

    

      
    
        
  • Be patient! Academic reading takes time. Prioritize comprehension over completion. Review everything at least twice.
    • 
        
  • Your research question is paramount! Keep your goal in mind as you evaluate sources.
    • 
        
  • Withhold initial judgement! Ensure you understand the author's argument before you form an opinion. You're allowed to disagree with an author!
    • 
        
  • Exercise skepticism! Academic articles always center on an argument. They have an agenda. Researchers are fallible. Mistakes are made. Knowledge evolves and advances, is lost and rediscovered.
    • 
        
  • Ask questions! What doesn't make sense? What's missing? What don't you understand?
    • 
      

    


    

      

        
Broad Strokes: The Big Picture

        
    
          
  • Take notes while you read!
            
      
              
    • Resist the temptation to read everything before recording anything.
      • 
              
    • Annotate! Print the article out and write directly on it; alternatively, use an annotation tool in your viewing software.
      • 
            
    
          
    • 
          
  • Start with structure!
            
      
              
    • Look over the entire article. Try to locate an abstract first, then move on to the introductory and concluding paragraphs or sections. Read them slowly and carefully, taking time to follow the argument's logic.
      • 
              
    • If an idea in these sections seems important, scan the article for more. Read corresponding sections or pages rather than the entire piece.
      • 
            
    
          
    • 
          
  • Be selective!
            
      
              
    • Focus on what's relevant to your research question. Not every page demands close attention and scrutiny.
      • 
              
    • Think critically about relationships. Strive for fluid transitions back and forth.
      • 
            
    
          
    • 
        

      

      

        
How to Read an Academic Article

        
    
          
  • Prioritize big ideas!
            
      
              
    • What are the author's main ideas? What larger points support them?
      • 
              
    • Don't mire yourself in extraneous details and evidence. Read, reflect, and keep moving forward.
      • 
            
    
          
    • 
          
  • Ask questions!
            
      
              
    • Your research question should guide your reading. As you move between paragraphs, record a question or two the next section might answer.
      • 
              
    • Pay close attention to the author's logic and tone. Add notes on their intent.
      • 
            
    
          
    • 
          
  • Summarize and paraphrase!
            
      
              
    • However you prefer to take notes, endeavor to answer your guiding questions through your own writing.
      • 
              
    • Restating an author's argument demonstrates your level of understanding.
      • 
            
    
          
    • 
          
  • Recite and review!
            
      
              
    • Read your notes back to yourself. Can you follow their logic?
      • 
              
    • Return to the article and fill in any gaps!
      • 
            
    
          
    •

Getting Started

    
Embrace the irony, children: academic articles cited in bibliographies aren’t always read in full. As you consider an article, proceed through these strategies to assess its worth.

    

      

        
First, Scan for Keywords

        
    
          
  • Search the article for the specific keywords you've deemed relevant.
    • 
          
  • Look for synonyms or alternative terms.
    • 
          
  • Can't find what you're interested in? Abandon the article and look elsewhere!
    • 
        

      

      

        
Second, Skim for Relevance

        
    
          
  • Read the first and last paragraphs.
    • 
          
  • Read the titles, headings, and subheadings.
    • 
          
  • Consider the visuals: image and caption.
    • 
          
  • Read the first sentence of each paragraph.
    • 
        

      

      

        
Third, Close Read for Understanding

        
    
          
  • Read the entire article.
    • 
          
  • Underline key ideas and information.
    • 
          
  • Annotate and take notes as you proceed.
    • 
          
  • Write down questions.
    • 
          
  • Consider relationships between what you know and what you're reading.
    • 
        

      

    


    
Some Notetaking Strategies

    
Want to go deeper? Consult On Annotation: A Handbook for Active Readers.

    
A few general tips at the top: Value your own perspective. Quality over quantity. Write on the page. Keep it simple. Don't highlight more than 5% of a page. Be kind to your future self.

    

      

        
Outlining

        
Outlines are composed of individual ideas identified by bullet points. They summarize an article's logic, with main ideas supported by subpoints and details. Outlining proves tricky if you struggle to identify main ideas.

      

      

        
Mapping

        
Mapping creates a visual representation of an article's main ideas. Directional cues and annotations convey argumentative logic and add details. Mapping tends to omit supporting concepts due to spatial constraints.

      

      

        
Sentencing

        
Sentencing represents ideas or concepts in individual sentences. If you struggle with outlining, sentencing can help you sort main and supporting ideas. When you're done reading, reorder your sentences to emulate the author's argument.

Reading News Articles


    
Appraising a News Article

    

      
    
        
  • Fact or opinion? Affective or informative journalism? Ideally, news offers fact-based reporting that conveys the who, what, when, where, why, and how of a story. Opinion pieces offer an author's perspective on a story or topic. Affective reporting appeals to emotion; informative reporting favors raw knowledge, firsthand accounts, and multiple versions of events.
    • 
        
  • Bulletin, long-form, or something in-between? Bulletins or short-form articles convey the facts of a story and nothing more. Long-form articles attempt to contextualize an event, often by making an argument. Styles and motives vary wildly! Many online news articles fall somewhere in the middle, offering some perspective alongside a version of events.
    • 
        
  • Read the headline! Read the lede! Dive in!
    • 
      

    


    
The Inverted Pyramid

    

      

        
The Inverted Pyramid style of journalism took off during the 19th century, buoyed by the advent of the telegraph and a push for objectivity in newspapers and magazines. The format is easy to read and easy to write! It places:

        
    
          
  • Essential information at the top: who, what, when, where, why, and how.
    • 
          
  • Supporting information in the middle: evidence, context, connections, etc.
    • 
          
  • Inessential information at the bottom: anecdotes, interpretation, etc.
    • 
        

        
A note on that origin story: the telegraph explanation is widely repeated but apocryphal and disputed. Poynter calls it a popular myth, and a content analysis of the New York Herald and New York Times found the form became common only in the 1880s, well after the telegraph (Pottker, Journalism Studies, 2003). Commerce, professional norms, and editing convenience likely mattered as much as the wire.

      

      

        

          
Most important information leads the story

          
Subsequent paragraphs give additional facts

          
Least important information closes the story
